Lecture1: The Column Space of AAA Contains All Vectors AxAxAx

MIT 18.065: Matrix Methods in Data Analysis, Signal Processing, and Machine Learning

this note is only a list of the key points

matrix times vector

there are two ways to cumpute matrix times vector

  • dot products (row)⋅\cdot⋅xxx <low level way>
  • vectorwise

[2133145712]⋅[x1x2x3]=x1⋅[235]+x2⋅[117]+x3⋅[3412]\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 3 \\ 3 & 1 & 4 \\ 5 & 7 & 12 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} x_1\\x_2\\x_3 \end{bmatrix}= x_1\cdot \begin{bmatrix} 2\\3\\5 \end{bmatrix}+x_2\cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1\\1\\7 \end{bmatrix}+x_3\cdot \begin{bmatrix} 3\\4\\12 \end{bmatrix} ⎣⎡​235​117​3412​⎦⎤​⋅⎣⎡​x1​x2​x3​​⎦⎤​=x1​⋅⎣⎡​235​⎦⎤​+x2​⋅⎣⎡​117​⎦⎤​+x3​⋅⎣⎡​3412​⎦⎤​

the core of the computation is to get the linear combination of vectors (columns of AAA)

column space C(A)C(A)C(A)

If the x=[x1x2x3...]x=\begin{bmatrix} x_1\\x_2\\x_3\\... \end{bmatrix}x=⎣⎢⎢⎡​x1​x2​x3​...​⎦⎥⎥⎤​ can be any vector, all the AxAxAxs form a space called the column space of AAA, noted C(A)C(A)C(A).

Take x=[x1x2x3]x=\begin{bmatrix} x_1\\x_2\\x_3 \end{bmatrix}x=⎣⎡​x1​x2​x3​​⎦⎤​ as an example. If all the columns in AAA are independent, then the column space is a R3\R^3R3 which is a space. However, otherwise, it may be a plane (R2\R^2R2) or a line (R\RR).

This definition helps to do the sampling. If you want a vector in C(A)C(A)C(A), DO NOT randomly pick a column. Instead, randomly generate a vector xxx and AxAxAx is the combination of columns which is a vector in C(A)C(A)C(A).

row space R(A)R(A)R(A) (or C(AT)C(A^T)C(AT))

Similar to the column space, all combinations of the rows form the row space. Therefore, R(A)=C(AT)R(A) = C(A^T)R(A)=C(AT).

independent columns and rows

The independent columns are the bases of the C(A)C(A)C(A). The independent rows are the bases of the C(AT)C(A^T)C(AT).

A=C⋅RA = C \cdot RA=C⋅R

AAA can be decomposed into CCC, the independent columns directly taken from AAA, and RRR, the row reduced echlon form of the matrix.
 

[2133145712]=[213157]⋅[101011]A=C⋅R\begin{aligned} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 3 \\ 3 & 1 & 4 \\ 5 & 7 & 12 \end{bmatrix}&= \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 1 \\ 5 & 7 \end{bmatrix}\cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \\[1.8em] A&=\kern{1.3em}C \kern{1.3em}\cdot \kern{2em}R \end{aligned} ⎣⎡​235​117​3412​⎦⎤​A​=⎣⎡​235​117​⎦⎤​⋅[10​01​11​]=C⋅R​

col1col1col1 in RRR means col1A=1⋅col1C+0⋅col2Ccol1_A = 1 \cdot col1_C + 0 \cdot col2_Ccol1A​=1⋅col1C​+0⋅col2C​ So does the other columns in RRR.

row1row1row1 in CCC means row1A=2⋅row1R+0⋅row2Rrow1_A = 2 \cdot row1_R + 0 \cdot row2_Rrow1A​=2⋅row1R​+0⋅row2R​ So does the other rows in CCC.

In this way, we decomposed AAA into C⋅RC \cdot RC⋅R which reveals the relation of the ranks:

# of cols in C=rankcol=# of rows in R=rankrow\# \ of \ cols \ in \ C = rank_{col} = \# \ of \ rows \ in \ R = rank_{row} # of cols in C=rankcol​=# of rows in R=rankrow​

In this example, rankcol=rankrow=2rank_{col} = rank_{row} = 2rankcol​=rankrow​=2.
